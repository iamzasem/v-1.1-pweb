German Present Tense Conjugation: A Beginner’s Guide

In German, conjugation means changing a verb to match the subject (who is doing the action). In the present tense, verbs adjust their endings or forms based on subjects like ich (I), du (you, informal singular), er/sie/es (he/she/it), wir (we), ihr (you, informal plural), and sie/Sie (they/you formal). There are two main types of verbs in German present tense: regular verbs, which follow a predictable pattern, and irregular verbs, which have unique changes that you need to memorize.

This guide focuses only on the present tense and includes:

  • A detailed explanation of regular verb conjugation.
  • An explanation of irregular verb conjugation, including common patterns and exceptions.
  • 20 examples of regular verbs and 20 examples of irregular verbs with conjugations and sentences.
  • Practice questions to test your skills.

Let’s dive in!

1. Regular Verbs in Present Tense

Regular verbs in German are easy to conjugate because they follow a consistent pattern. Here’s how it works:

  1. Find the stem: Take the infinitive form of the verb (e.g., spielen = to play) and remove the -en ending. This leaves you with the stem (spiel-).
  2. Add endings: Attach the appropriate ending to the stem based on the subject.

Regular Verb Conjugation Pattern

Subject Ending Example: spielen (to play)
ich (I)-espiele
du (you, informal singular)-stspielst
er/sie/es (he/she/it)-tspielt
wir (we)-enspielen
ihr (you, informal plural)-tspielt
sie/Sie (they/you formal)-enspielen

Notes on Regular Verbs

  • The -en ending stays the same as the infinitive for wir and sie/Sie.
  • For verbs ending in -ten or -den (like arbeiten = to work), add an extra -e- before the -st and -t endings (e.g., du arbeitest, er arbeitet).
  • For verbs ending in -eln or -ern (like wandern = to hike), the ich form often drops the -e- in the stem (e.g., ich wandre), but this is optional for beginners.

2. Irregular Verbs in Present Tense

Irregular verbs don’t follow the regular pattern fully. They often change their stem vowel for du and er/sie/es, while the other forms (ich, wir, ihr, sie/Sie) may look more regular. Some irregular verbs, like sein (to be), have completely unique forms.

Irregular Verb Patterns

  • e → i: nehmen (to take) → du nimmst.
  • e → ie: sehen (to see) → du siehst.
  • a → ä: fahren (to drive) → du fährst.
  • Unique forms: Verbs like sein and haben don’t follow any pattern and must be memorized.

Example of an Irregular Verb

Subject Conjugation: sehen (to see)
ichsehe
dusiehst
er/sie/essieht
wirsehen
ihrseht
sie/Siesehen

The vowel changes from e to ie for du and er/sie/es.

Final Thoughts

Regular Verbs: Stick to the pattern (-e, -st, -t, -en, -t, -en), and you’ll master them quickly.
Irregular Verbs: Focus on memorizing the stem vowel changes (e.g., e → i, a → ä) and unique forms like sein and haben.
Practice: Use these verbs in sentences, make flashcards, or try speaking them aloud to build confidence.

Learning German conjugation takes time, but with regular practice, you’ll get there. Keep going, and viel Spaß beim Lernen! (Have fun learning!)
