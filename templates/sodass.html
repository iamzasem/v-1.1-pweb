So...dass and Sodass in German

A guide to the German consequential conjunctions "so...dass" and "sodass," explaining their meanings ("so...that," "such that") and usage, with examples, exercises, and resources.

What Do "So...dass" and "Sodass" Mean?

"So...dass" and "sodass" connect a cause or condition to its result, with the verb at the end of the subordinate clause.

Key Points

  • So...dass: "So...that" with so (degree) and dass (result) (e.g., Es ist so kalt, dass ich friere.).
  • Sodass: "So that" as one word (e.g., Es ist kalt, sodass ich friere.).
  • Structure: Main clause + comma + subordinate clause (verb-end).

Differences Between "So...dass" and "Sodass"

Structure and Focus

So...dass emphasizes degree with two parts; sodass links cause and effect directly.

Uses of "So...dass" and "Sodass"

Basic: Showing Consequences

  1. Es ist so laut, dass ich nicht schlafe. (It’s so loud that I don’t sleep.)

Intermediate: Emphasizing Degree

  1. Sie spricht so leise, dass ich nichts verstehe. (She speaks so quietly that I don’t understand anything.)

Advanced: Formal Writing

  1. Die Maschine läuft leise, sodass niemand klagt. (The machine runs quietly, so that no one complains.)

Tips to Master "So...dass" and "Sodass"

  • Focus on Degree: Use so...dass with adjectives (so kalt).
  • Simplify with Sodass: Use sodass for direct results.
  • Practice Verb-End: Keep verbs at the end of subordinate clauses.
  • Mix and Match: Rewrite sentences with both.
